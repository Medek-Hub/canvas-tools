1. Concept Anchor StatementBefore you pick the drug, find the failure.

Respiratory and allergic emergency medications only make sense when you connect them to the patient’s failing physiology.
EMTs recognize the patient is short of breath, wheezing, swollen, itchy, pale, anxious, or hypotensive.
Paramedics recognize airway edema, bronchospasm, mucus plugging, air trapping, vasodilation, capillary leak, shock, and respiratory fatigue before the patient completely crashes.

Week 2 is where medication memorization begins turning into medication reasoning. It is not enough to know that albuterol is “for wheezing” or that epinephrine is “for anaphylaxis.” A paramedic asks: What part of the patient is failing right now? Is the airway swelling shut? Are the bronchi constricting? Is the patient trapping air? Is ventilation failing? Is the vascular system leaking and dilating? Is the patient still compensating, or are they about to fall off the cliff?

The same patient can have more than one failure at the same time. A severe anaphylaxis patient may have airway edema, bronchospasm, vasodilation, capillary leak, hypotension, and anxiety all at once. That is why epinephrine is such a high-value medication in anaphylaxis: it targets multiple failures, not just one symptom.

2. Concept ExplanationRespiratory distress is not one problem. It is a race between oxygenation, ventilation, and fatigue.

Respiratory patients can look similar from the doorway, but the failure inside may be different. Oxygenation is about getting oxygen into the blood. Ventilation is about moving carbon dioxide out. Work of breathing is about how hard the body is working to do both. A patient can have a decent SpO₂ for a while and still be ventilating poorly, especially if they are trapping air or getting exhausted.

Asthma and COPD commonly involve lower-airway narrowing. In asthma, the classic problem is an inflammatory airway that overreacts: bronchospasm, swelling, and mucus reduce airflow. In COPD, chronic airway damage and loss of elastic recoil can make exhalation difficult, causing air trapping and increased dead space. Either way, the patient may be moving less effective air with more effort.

The EMS medication goal is not just “make the wheeze quieter.” The goal is to improve airflow, reduce bronchoconstriction, support oxygenation, support ventilation, and prevent fatigue from becoming respiratory failure. If the patient is too tired to breathe, a nebulizer alone is not enough; ventilation support becomes the treatment.

Diagram 2 — Tight airways are a mechanical and inflammatory problem.

Assessment clues that matter

  • Can the patient speak in full sentences?
  • Is wheezing loud, faint, or suddenly absent?
  • Are accessory muscles working hard?
  • Is EtCO₂ rising, falling, or showing a shark-fin pattern?
  • Is the patient improving after medication, or just getting quieter?

Do not let SpO₂ fool you

SpO₂ tells you oxygen saturation, not the full story of ventilation or fatigue. A patient can have acceptable SpO₂ while carbon dioxide is rising, especially if oxygen is being delivered but ventilation is failing. Look at the patient, the waveform, the respiratory effort, and the trend.

3. Receptor MapReceptors are the buttons medications press — and each button has a price.

Most Week 2 medications are easier once you understand the receptor target. A beta-2 agonist is not “a breathing medicine” by magic; it relaxes bronchial smooth muscle. Epinephrine is not “strong Benadryl”; it stimulates alpha and beta receptors to reverse life-threatening airway and perfusion problems. Anticholinergics do not directly stimulate beta receptors; they reduce parasympathetic bronchoconstriction. Antihistamines do not reopen shocky blood vessels or fix severe airway edema; they block histamine effects and are supportive.

4. Medication PMS CardsWhat each medication is trying to fix

Use these cards as your “why” before you study the dose. Doses matter, but dose memorization without mechanism is like knowing the address without knowing why you were dispatched.

Oxygen

Supports oxygenation

P: Hypoxemia or high-risk respiratory distress.

M: Increases oxygen available for diffusion into blood.

S: Reassess SpO₂, mental status, work of breathing, and signs of worsening ventilation. Oxygen does not fix bronchospasm by itself.

Albuterol / Levalbuterol

Beta-2 bronchodilation

P: Wheezing, bronchospasm, asthma/COPD exacerbation.

M: Stimulates beta-2 receptors, relaxing bronchial smooth muscle and improving airflow.

S: Watch tachycardia, tremor, palpitations, anxiety, and poor response. Reassess lung sounds, ability to speak, EtCO₂ waveform, RR, and fatigue.

Ipratropium

Blocks parasympathetic constriction

P: Bronchospasm with cholinergic contribution, especially COPD/asthma exacerbations.

M: Blocks muscarinic receptors in the airway, reducing bronchoconstriction.

S: Useful with beta-2 agonists. Watch drying effects and understand it is not the same mechanism as albuterol.

Epinephrine

The multi-tool for anaphylaxis

P: Anaphylaxis with airway swelling, bronchospasm, hypotension, or shock risk.

M: Alpha-1 vasoconstriction supports BP and reduces edema; beta-1 supports cardiac output; beta-2 bronchodilates.

S: Time matters. Reassess BP, airway swelling, wheezing, skin signs, mental status, and recurrence. Watch tachycardia, hypertension, chest pain, and dysrhythmias.

Diphenhydramine

H1 symptom support

P: Hives, itching, allergic symptoms; adjunct in anaphylaxis after the life threat is addressed.

M: Blocks H1 histamine receptors, reducing some allergic effects.

S: Sedation and anticholinergic effects can confuse reassessment. It does not replace epinephrine for anaphylaxis.

Famotidine / H2 Blocker

Supportive histamine blockade

P: Allergic reaction adjunct when included in local protocol.

M: Blocks H2 receptors; may support broader histamine blockade.

S: Helpful as an adjunct, but it does not fix airway edema, bronchospasm, or shock by itself.

Corticosteroids

Inflammation control over time

P: Airway inflammation, asthma/COPD flare, allergic inflammation, risk of prolonged symptoms.

M: Reduces inflammatory signaling and mediator production, but onset is not immediate rescue.

S: Do not expect instant bronchodilation. Watch BGL, immune considerations, and remember they support later control.

Magnesium Sulfate

Smooth muscle relaxation support

P: Severe/refractory bronchospasm per protocol.

M: Can help relax bronchial smooth muscle and reduce severe airway resistance.

S: Monitor BP, respiratory status, ECG, and overall response. This is not your first move for every wheeze.

Racemic / Nebulized Epinephrine

Upper airway swelling support

P: Stridor or upper-airway edema/croup-like presentation per protocol.

M: Local alpha effect can reduce mucosal swelling; beta effect may support airflow.

S: Reassess stridor, work of breathing, oxygenation, and rebound symptoms. Prepare for escalation if the airway worsens.

⚠ High Risk Concept: A patient can sound “quieter” because treatment worked — or because they are too fatigued to move air. Never reassess with your ears only. Reassess the whole patient.

5. Allergic Reaction vs AnaphylaxisAnaphylaxis is a system-wide failure, not a bad rash.

An allergic reaction can be local and uncomfortable. Anaphylaxis is different because it threatens airway, breathing, circulation, or more than one body system. Mast cells and basophils release chemical mediators that produce vasodilation, capillary leak, bronchospasm, mucus production, and swelling. The patient may be itchy and flushed, but the dangerous part is what you cannot ignore: airway narrowing, worsening ventilation, and shock.

When anaphylaxis appears, epinephrine is the medication that most directly matches the life-threatening physiology. Antihistamines and steroids can be useful adjuncts, but they are not the rescue medication for airway edema, bronchospasm, and distributive shock. In simple words: Benadryl may calm the histamine party, but epinephrine stops the building from collapsing.

Diagram 5 — Anaphylaxis is a cascade, not a single symptom.
Mechanism → Physiology → Outcome Chain
  1. Allergen exposure triggers immune mediator release.
  2. Mediators cause vasodilation, capillary leak, bronchospasm, mucus, and airway swelling.
  3. The patient develops respiratory compromise, hypotension, skin findings, GI symptoms, or altered mental status.
  4. Epinephrine stimulates alpha and beta receptors to improve vascular tone, airway edema, cardiac output, and bronchodilation.
  5. Adjuncts such as antihistamines and steroids may help symptoms or delayed inflammation, but reassessment and transport remain essential.

6. Field ApplicationNASEMSO-style thinking: treat, trend, document, reassess.

National model guidance reinforces that respiratory care is not “give a nebulizer and move on.” The EMS clinician should identify respiratory distress, respiratory failure, and respiratory arrest early, then escalate therapy as needed. In practical terms, that means you keep asking: Is this patient moving enough air to stay alive?

Respiratory Distress/Asthma/COPD/Reactive Airway

  • Document baseline respiratory exam before intervention.
  • Trend RR, work of breathing, lung sounds, SpO₂, EtCO₂, mental status, and ability to speak.
  • Use medications to reduce bronchospasm when indicated.
  • Escalate to assisted ventilation when medication is not enough.

Allergy/Anaphylaxis

  • Look for airway swelling, wheezing, respiratory distress, hypotension, and shock.
  • Treat life threats first; epinephrine timing matters.
  • Use oxygen and ventilation support based on the patient’s respiratory status.
  • Reassess after each intervention and monitor for recurrence.

Documentation drill

After each medication, chart what changed: breathing effort, lung sounds, SpO₂, EtCO₂ waveform, BP, HR, skin, mental status, and patient speech. If nothing changed, that is also important data.

7. Failure Cascade ExplanationWhat happens if we miss the physiology?

If bronchospasm, swelling, and mucus continue untreated, the patient works harder to move less air. As air trapping worsens, ventilation becomes inefficient, carbon dioxide may rise, and respiratory muscles begin to fatigue. Eventually, the patient may stop wheezing not because they improved, but because airflow is too poor to create wheezes.

If anaphylaxis progresses untreated, vascular tone falls, capillaries leak, airway swelling worsens, and bronchospasm increases resistance to airflow. The patient can move from anxiety and hives to hypotension, hypoxia, altered mental status, and arrest. The danger is speed: anaphylaxis can deteriorate faster than the class notes can keep up.

⚠ High Risk Concept: A delayed epinephrine decision in anaphylaxis is not a small delay. It gives airway edema, bronchospasm, and shock more time to win.

8. Clinical Mindset Check Scenario🚑 “The Wheeze That Got Quiet”

Dispatch: 22-year-old female at a park after a food exposure, now short of breath and “feeling weird.” Friends report she has a peanut allergy.

BP 88/54
HR 132
RR 32, labored
Temp 37.1°C / 98.8°F
SpO₂ 91%
EtCO₂ 48, shark-fin waveform
BGL 104 mg/dL
Skin flushed, hives

Exam: Swelling to lips, hoarse voice, diffuse wheezing that becomes quieter while the patient becomes more tired. She is anxious, speaking 2–3 word sentences, and now has delayed cap refill.

Think like a paramedic.

  1. What is failing inside this patient?
  2. Which medication directly matches the airway, breathing, and circulation threat?
  3. Which medications are adjuncts, and why are they not the priority rescue medication?
  4. What reassessment findings would tell you the patient is improving?
  5. What findings would tell you to escalate ventilation or airway management?
Instructor reasoning notes

This patient has anaphylaxis with airway edema, bronchospasm, hypotension, and likely worsening ventilatory failure. Epinephrine is the priority medication because alpha-1, beta-1, and beta-2 effects match the life threats. Albuterol may help bronchospasm, antihistamines may support histamine symptoms, and steroids may support delayed inflammatory control, but none replace early epinephrine and airway/ventilation support.
